If you’ve been online at all in recent years, you know that generative AI is inescapable. Almost every social media platform has some sort of artificial intelligence hard baked into it, whether it be AI summaries or an online chatbot. AI generated multimedia is becoming more prevalent online, gaining millions of views each day, despite creators openly protesting against the automation of their careers. Students are openly using generative AI to complete assignments, leaving many teachers scrambling to counter. Companies across the world are embracing AI in hopes of improving productivity and lowering costs, all while collaborating with militaristic governments aiming to be on top in a cowardly new world. Given recent developments, it begs the question: how did we get here?

In 1950, Alan Turing (1912–1954) published “Computing Machinery and Intelligence”, in which he outlines the basis for an “imitation game”, later iterations of which would be named after himself. The term “artificial intelligence” itself would be coined at the [Dartmouth Summer Research Project on Artificial Intelligence](https://home.dartmouth.edu/about/artificial-intelligence-ai-coined-dartmouth) in 1956, on the assumption that “every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it.”

In 1966, Joseph Weizenbaum at MIT would publish his work on the [AI chatbot ELIZA](https://www.masswerk.at/elizabot/). It was designed as a “general conversational agent” that could interpret different, more specialized scripts. The most famous of these was the DOCTOR script, where the chatbot took on the role of a psychiatrist. Following this, the field of artificial intelligence stayed dormant in the public eye for several decades as the limits of computational logistics lagged behind the hopes and goals of scientists, though advancements were still being made in the form of modern backpropagation and neural networks.

AI would regain prominence in 1997, when [IBM’s Deep Blue beat World Champion Garry Kasparov](https://www.ibm.com/history/deep-blue) at chess, and later again in 2011 with IBM Watson’s victory on the *Jeopardy\!* game show. 2014 saw the concept of generative adversarial networks, where two neural networks compete against each other to create new, realistic media. In what has been described as a repeat of Deep Blue vs. Kasparov, DeepMind’s AlphaGo defeated top Go player Lee Sedol 4–1 in March 2016\. In 2017, Google researchers published the paper “[Attention Is All You Need](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762)”, outlining a novel deep learning architecture, the transformer, which became the basis behind most modern generative AI models today. 

AI development has continued to accelerate with OpenAI's release of GPT-1 (2018), GPT-2 (2018), and GPT-3 (2020) large language models, followed by the DALL-E 1 (2021) text-to-image model, which paved the way for other image generation models like Midjourney (2022) and Stability AI's Stable Diffusion (2022). This, along with the release of OpenAI's ChatGPT in 2022, marked the beginning of the current AI boom. 2023 saw the release of GPT-4, Meta's LLaMA 1, and Google's Bard (later renamed Gemini), while 2024 saw the release of OpenAI's Sora text-to-video model (despite the shutdown in 2026, other text-to-video models have sprung up in its place), AI overviews for Google searches, and Apple Intelligence for new iPhones.

While the underlying technology of generative AI has evolved at a breakneck pace [with abilities  doubling every couple of months](https://theaidigest.org/time-horizons), our regulations and ability to adapt has not. With the rapid integration of these models into our lives, AI regulation has gone from the type of technical novelty only seen in books and film to a [vital matter of privacy and safety](https://www.ibm.com/think/insights/ai-privacy). The question is whether our governments understand the risks of progress without boundaries or if we dive head first into a society dependent on technology we don’t fully understand.

Here in the United States, the government has taken a very market-centric approach focusing on “staying ahead” of our supposed competition abroad and not putting in place any harsh corporate restrictions. Washington has avoided creating a dedicated federal AI regulator. Instead, the White House issued its [National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/03.20.26-National-Policy-Framework-for-Artificial-Intelligence-Legislative-Recommendations.pdf), advocating for a voluntary compliance model developed in partnership with tech companies such as Palantir or OpenAI. Federal policy has also limited individual U.S. states from passing their own AI safety laws that could slow down American tech development.

This is not the only path, however, and our friends across the pond in the European Union have focused on a stricter and more precautionary approach. With the [EU Artificial Intelligence Act](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/), Brussels established a rigid regulatory framework that categorizes AI applications by their level of risk. Under this law, unacceptable risks such as government-run social scoring and biometric categorization are prohibited, and high risk items including but not limited to education grading, job recruitment screening, profiling individuals in criminal investigations, and infrastructure management must be under human supervision.

A variety of political scientists and politicians have increasingly described an "[AI arms race](https://www.csis.org/analysis/protecting-our-edge-trade-secrets-and-global-ai-arms-race)" between global superpowers, primarily the United States and China. This framing treats the development of complex algorithms with real social implications as something to be accelerated for the sake of national security, even when it could lead to disastrous implications. Outside of highly specialized, life-saving fields like [medical imaging](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10740686/) or [protein folding](https://www.quantamagazine.org/how-ai-revolutionized-protein-science-but-didnt-end-it-20240626/), the hyper-accelerated deployment of consumer generative AI introduces unprecedented risks to global stability. Yet, the rush to build bigger models continues unabated. Control over the AI supply chain, including the semiconductor chips, memory and energy needed to make truly enormous AI data centers, has become a leverage point in foreign policy. 

The battle lines are clearly drawn around hardware. Bipartisan groups of U.S. senators have actively pushed the White House to tighten regulations on manufacturers like [Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC)](https://www.tsmc.com/english), which controls roughly [90% of the advanced chip market](https://thenextweb.com/news/tsmc-price-rise-inflation-ai-chips-taiwan-advanced-manufacturing), to block Chinese subsidiaries from exploiting loopholes. Massive AI clusters demand immense amounts of electricity, stretching municipal power grids to their absolute breaking point. A 2026 United Nations University report revealed that global data centers consumed an astonishing [448 terawatt-hours (TWh) of electricity in 2025 alone](https://unu.edu/inweh/news/environmental-cost-of-AIs-Enrgy-use-carbon-water-and-land-footprints), a figure that rivaled the [entire power consumption of France](https://www.iea.org/countries/france/electricity).

These AI systems are being pushed by for-profit corporations which prioritize their own interests over ours. Building, training, and running frontier AI models requires an astronomical amount of capital that only a few tech titans possess. A few consolidated megacorporations: OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, Amazon, Meta and Microsoft, are essentially pushing the entire market forward in the hopes that this isn’t a bubble and they can be the first to achieve [Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)](https://www.ibm.com/think/topics/artificial-general-intelligence). Because hundreds of enterprises now rely on the exact same core models and software vendors, a phenomenon known as an "algorithmic monoculture" has emerged. If an AI model harbors a systemic flaw or bias, that flaw is instantly replicated across the entire economy, locking out vulnerable groups at an unprecedented scale.

In the secretive halls of the Pentagon, private defense tech firms have positioned themselves as the irreplaceable nervous system of modern warfare. Chief among these is Palantir Technologies. Long known for its data-mining software, the company has scaled its footprint within global defense infrastructure to an unprecedented degree. [The U.S. Army awarded Palantir a massive $10 billion enterprise agreement](https://www.army.mil/article/287506/u_s_army_awards_enterprise_service_agreement_to_enhance_military_readiness_and_drive_operational_efficiency) to consolidate its data and software architectures under a single commercial vendor. Furthermore, the Pentagon formalized Palantir’s Maven Smart System (MSS), which began as a controversial drone-imagery analysis project, into an official, long-term "program of record" under the Chief Digital and Artificial Intelligence Office (CDAIO).

We didn’t vote to have generative AI summarize our private emails or replace our writing and style; this state of affairs has been forced upon us in a massive campaign to make AI an integral part of our new reality. Yet, this corporate-driven and unregulated trajectory is not an inevitability. An alternative path exists if we choose to fund and champion public interest technology and put in place actual, binding regulation. Technology is not a rogue wave washing over humanity; it’s a reflection of ourselves. We have to accept that given the chance, private entities and governments will not use these systems for our benefit. If we want a future where technology elevates humanity rather than exploiting it, we must stop acting like passive spectators in an arms race we never agreed to join.
