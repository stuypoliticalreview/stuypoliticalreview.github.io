## **Introduction**
On February 24, 2022—incidentally, my fourteenth birthday—Russia launched a “special military operation” against Ukraine. After the fallout of the Orange Revolution, Ukraine had been slowly clawing its way out of the bear’s paws, drifting towards the European Union and North Atlantic Treaty Organization. Using the pretext of [“denazification” and “demilitarization,”](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cj0q964851po) Russia invaded Ukraine, sparking a four-year-long war that has cost millions of lives and counting.  

However, this is not an article about Ukraine. Rather, this will focus on other Eastern European countries and how post-Soviet and Russian influence still lingers and affects the politics of their nations. Many nations, ranging from struggling, corrupt hybrid democracies such as Armenia to blossoming e-democracies like Estonia, still face the consequences of a Russian legacy that refuses to shake itself from the lives of their populaces.

## **Fall of a Tyranny**
It is impossible to discuss Russia without mentioning its predecessor, the Soviet Union. Mere moments after its birth, the Bolshevik state engulfed itself into wars against the Baltic states, Belarus, Ukraine, Finland, and Poland. Some, like Finland and Poland (notable for its “Miracle on the Vistula”), were able to resist the Soviet advances, while Belarus and Ukraine were annexed into the fledgling empire.  

After World War II, the Soviets would grow their sphere of influence to encompass the entirety of Eastern Europe, save for Yugoslavia. Dissent in Soviet satellite states was crushed; in Prague, 1968, attempts at reform were [squashed](https://thenonviolenceproject.wisc.edu/2023/03/24/seven-days-that-ended-the-prague-spring/) by the Soviet Army, while inside 1956 Hungary, a democratic revolution was brutally suppressed. Despite this, resistance still accumulated, and by the 1980s, the Soviet Union was on the decline and unable to manage these protests. A combination of [factors](https://www.britannica.com/story/why-did-the-soviet-union-collapse), ranging from the intended purpose of *glasnost* failing to crashing oil prices to disasters such as the invasion of Afghanistan and Chernobyl spelled the end of the Soviet Union. By the turn of the millennium, the Soviet Union was nothing but a memory of the past.

Despite the Soviet Union having fallen thirty-five years ago, its successor, Russia, has continually exerted its power amongst the post-Warsaw Pact nations through a variety of means. Claims of election interference, bribes, and political violence are rife in many Eastern European states. I will now look at a number of case studies of Russian pervasive influence in Eastern Europe.

## **The Baltics: Weaponization of a People**
The three Baltic sisters of Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania were some of the first to break free of the Soviet clutches and have undergone significant transformations, drawing themselves closer to the West. Estonia, in particular, has embraced the internet in furthering its democracy, garnering international attention for its weaving of the digital world into its electoral practices. However, it and Latvia have significant Russian populations–almost [a quarter](https://europeanperspective.news/russians-in-the-baltics-european-minorities-chapter-2/) of Estonians and Latvians are ethnically Russian.

The Russian population in the Baltics sits in an awkward position. Traditionally, the diaspora has been a vestige of Soviet times, somewhat akin to Yugoslavians–nostalgia for the U.S.S.R., still seeing it as their homeland. However, while much of the diaspora still holds Russian culture close to their heart, most no longer view Russia as their homeland.  

This Russian diaspora has also been heavily invested in by the Russian state, with over [400 million rubles](https://www.lse.ac.uk/ideas/Assets/Documents/updates/LSE-IDEAS-Russian-Diaspora-Baltic-States.pdf) (roughly 6 million USD) being poured into various state-sponsored institutions. These foundations are, in a way, crucial to the Russian minorities, as there is much notable discrimination against them (Latvia has drawn [criticism](https://www.ohchr.org/en/press-releases/2023/02/latvia-un-experts-concerned-about-severe-curtailment-minority-language) due to its removal of Russian as a language of instruction); however, they are also an unsubtle method of gaining soft-power influence over the Baltic region.

## **Georgia: Georgian Nightmare**
The mountainous nation of Georgia provides one of the most ostentatious displays of Russian influence over foreign governments (other than the quasi-satellite state of Lukashenko’s Belarus). Unlike the Baltic states, Georgia does not have a sizable Russian minority; however, its minorities—the Abkhazians and Ossetians—have become Russia’s substitutes. These two minorities were under the control of the Georgian Soviet Socialist Republic, but in the aftermath of the Soviet dissolution, [attempted to secede](https://www.peaceagreements.org/conflict/58/) and form their own nations.   

These independence movements were heavily suppressed by the new Georgian government, leading the separatists to accept the help of Russia. To these people, aligning with the Russians was a necessary sacrifice in order to preserve their autonomy. They declared independence in 1991, breaking away officially from Georgia, although with extremely limited international recognition.  

Georgia’s [lack of investment](https://pesd.princeton.edu/node/706) has further drawn the two states towards Russia. In the absence of this support, Russia has stepped in, dedicating [nine billion rubles](https://www.state.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/Update-to-the-Report-on-Actions-Taken-by-the-Russian-Federation-Accessible-12.10.2025.pdf) to South Ossetia alone between 2018 and 2022\. Russia has also constructed significant barriers between the regions and Georgia proper, further isolating them from Tbilisi.  

Russian meddling in Abkhazia and South Ossetia culminated in the Russo-Georgian War, the first of Russia’s many incursions into other European nations (namely, Ukraine). The war was a resounding defeat for Georgia; however, what was most concerning was the inaction shown by the West. A [ceasefire](https://www.securitycouncilreport.org/atf/cf/%7B65BFCF9B-6D27-4E9C-8CD3-CF6E4FF96FF9%7D/GeorgiaSarkozyLetter.pdf) negotiated by Nicholas Sarkozy, the then-French president, called for a withdrawal of Russian forces. Russia, predictably, did not, which was met with silence by Western leaders. This silence has not only had catastrophic results for Georgia—it has alerted Russia that the West will not go further than strongly-worded letters of condemnation to oppose Russian actions in the Caucasus…*and Ukraine*.  

“Peacekeeping” is another facet of Russian influence in Georgia’s breakaway regions. Mandated as part (actually, the only part) of a CIS (Council of Independent States) operation, Russian peacekeepers have [operated](https://www.ui.se/globalassets/ui.se-eng/publications/sceeus/georgia-and-the-russian-aggression-dj.pdf) in Georgia since 1994\. These have been used as a shield by Russia to cover for its humanitarian abuses in Georgia and discredit the Georgian government.  

These delegitimizations of Georgian sovereignty are a dangerous model of what could be in Ukraine, but the worst could still be to come for Georgia.  

That is because of the “Georgian Dream.” The premier political party in Georgia, Georgian Dream has led the country since 2012, founded by oligarch Bidzina Ivanishvili. The party, despite Ivanishvili’s Russian business dealings, marketed itself as a pro-European political party, even joining the Party of European Socialists in [2015](https://georgiatoday.ge/georgian-dream-to-leave-the-party-of-european-socialists/).  

This facade has faded since its inception, with Ivanishvili using the party to [appease many of his Moscow connections](https://www.lowyinstitute.org/the-interpreter/georgian-dream-now-nothing-nightmare). This is despite a [near-90%](https://www.iri.org/news/iri-georgia-poll-finds-support-for-eu-accession-high-weariness-of-russian-presence-lack-of-faith-in-political-parties/) proportion of Georgians who support joining the European Union. Even after Ivanishvili’s term as prime minister ended, his influence has [loomed](https://www.osw.waw.pl/en/publikacje/osw-commentary/2024-10-08/a-journey-a-glorious-past-three-terms-georgian-dream) over the party like a haunting shadow. The party has repeatedly echoed propagandic claims such as the existence of a “[deep state](https://jamestown.org/georgian-dream-shifting-from-western-partner-to-provocateur/)” and decrying [several leaders](https://civil.ge/archives/669106) as agents of such from the European Union and United States.  

Georgian Dream’s pivot to Russia has not gone without consequences; political efficacy is at an all-time low. Additionally, numerous political crises have struck the party, including one in 2023 that pitted the (formerly Georgian Dream) president against the Georgian Dream-controlled Parliament. Salome Zourabchvili, the president, disagreed with the ruling party on their pro-Russian stances, and this, amongst other issues, led to her splintering from the party. The 2024 elections would see a very dubious result which Zourabchvili [contests](https://www.npr.org/2025/09/10/nx-s1-5534081/salome-zourabichvili-incumbent-president-of-georgia-refuses-to-stand-down) to this day. The election itself was [marred](https://odihr.osce.org/sites/default/files/f/documents/1/6/584029_0.pdf) by voter fraud, intimidation, propaganda, and more, leading to a fraudulent result criticized heavily by [international leaders](https://www.government.is/ministries/diplomatic-missions/embassy-article/2024/12/29/Joint-Statement-by-Nordic-Baltic-Foreign-Ministers-on-Situation-in-Georgia/).  

The 2024 elections were the breaking point for many Georgians, who flooded the streets of Tbilisi, Batumi, Kutaisi, and countless other cities, both in Georgia and [outside](https://www.radiotavisupleba.ge/a/%E1%83%94%E1%83%95%E1%83%A0%E1%83%9D%E1%83%9E%E1%83%90%E1%83%A1%E1%83%90-%E1%83%93%E1%83%90-%E1%83%90%E1%83%9B%E1%83%94%E1%83%A0%E1%83%98%E1%83%99%E1%83%90%E1%83%A8%E1%83%98-%E1%83%94%E1%83%9B%E1%83%98%E1%83%92%E1%83%A0%E1%83%90%E1%83%9C%E1%83%A2%E1%83%94%E1%83%91%E1%83%98%E1%83%A1-%E1%83%9E%E1%83%A0%E1%83%9D%E1%83%A2%E1%83%94%E1%83%A1%E1%83%A2%E1%83%98-%E1%83%90%E1%83%A0-%E1%83%AC%E1%83%A7%E1%83%93%E1%83%94%E1%83%91%E1%83%90/33230448.html) of it. The governmental response to these protests has produced only further discontent, with [allegations of torture and abuse](https://www.amnesty.org/en/petition/demand-accountability-justice-protesters-georgia/) towards protestors rampant and reports of tear gas being used. The protestors specifically [fight against](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c208w378gg7o) the pervasive Russian influence that has spread throughout Georgia like a pestilence, demanding a return to European integration and a restoration of a free and fair democracy.  

The 2024 protests still continue, [two years on](https://www.euronews.com/2026/05/27/thousands-in-georgia-rally-for-eu-membership-as-country-marks-35th-independence-anniversar)—the spirit of the demonstrators still rages like an inferno. Georgia may be a case of how deep Russian infiltration can go, but it is also an example of the brave resilience the people of a nation can show in the face of unimaginable odds, to defend their nation’s sovereignty.

## **Bulgaria: Oligarchical Rot**
Bulgaria, a country that has seen major political strife in the past few months, even being one of the many countries to undergo a “Gen-Z Revolution” and topple its government, has been plagued by an internal toxin. This toxin is the prevalence of Russian oligarchs. Historically, Bulgaria has been rather friendly with Russia. From Bulgaria’s rebirth in the 1870s, Russia has generally favored Bulgarian interests. Bulgaria, in return, has maintained a friendly foreign policy towards Russia, even refusing to declare war on the Soviet Union during World War II, despite being in the Axis Powers.   

This, unfortunately, still applies to this date, as Bulgaria is a notable hub for skirting sanctions imposed on Russia after it invaded Ukraine. An [investigation](https://sofiaglobe.com/2023/04/25/bulgaria-probes-claim-bank-is-laundering-money-for-russian-oligarchs/) was launched in 2023 after reports of alleged money laundering from an unnamed Bulgarian bank, possibly funneling funds to Russia using Hungary as an intermediary. Bulgaria has become such a major hub for money laundering that it has been [placed](https://www.rferl.org/a/fatf-bulgaria-money-laundering-monitoring/32656966.html) in the Financial Action Task Force’s “gray list” for financial corruption, in the [same league](https://www.fatf-gafi.org/en/countries/black-and-grey-lists.html) as Haiti, Angola, and the DRC. These schemes are often facilitated by Russian oligarchs, of whom Bulgaria hosts several. Some, such as Konstantin Malofeyev, have funneled more than just funds to Russia, but also [state secrets](https://www.rferl.org/a/russian-oligarch-malofeyev-banned-bulgaria-10-years-spy-scandal/30159179.html).  

In Bulgarian politics, Russophilia runs deep. Many Bulgarian oligarchs have established their own political parties, adopting pro-Russian stances. Even in the larger, more established parties, politicians are often reluctant to take real action against Russia. The Bulgarian Socialist Party, one of the largest and oldest parties in the nation, has been criticized for its soft Russophilia. Its leader, Kornelia Ninova, has said that Bulgaria, “[should improve ties with Moscow and defend its interests within the 28-member bloc](https://www.reuters.com/article/us-bulgaria-election-socialists/socialists-say-bulgaria-pays-high-price-for-eus-russia-sanctions-idUSKBN16O1AA/) \[EU\].” Bulgaria, along with Hungary, has also been one of the most reluctant countries in the European Union to lend military aid to Ukraine. This opposition has been led by some of its most influential politicians–even its [*president*](https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2022/11/04/bulgarian-mps-approve-military-aid-to-ukraine-despite-opposition), Rumen Radev, who only resigned this year in the wake of Bulgaria’s Gen-Z revolution, which saw much of the government resign.  

Radev and his party “Progressive Bulgaria” which is *slightly* more conservative than the name would suggest, has found success in the 2026 parliamentary elections. Radev has been criticized for harboring sentiments of Russophilia, even if  he may be an improvement from previous leadership. This still goes to show Russia’s deep stranglehold over Bulgaria and that it does not lie solely within its oligarchs, but in something else. It is an issue that affects not only Bulgaria, but also countless other countries in Europe, and is Russia’s ace: energy.

## **The Energy Crisis: A Faustian Bargain**
The outbreak of the Russo-Ukrainian War plunged Europe into crisis. Nearly half of Europe’s gas and a quarter of its oil [originated](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/10/3/how-much-of-europes-oil-and-gas-still-comes-from-russia) in Russia prior to the war. The price of oil and other critical resources spiked in the aftermath of the Russian invasion, leading to an energy crisis that hadn’t been seen since the oil crisis of the 1980s.  

To this end, the European Union has taken action. Three months after the invasion of Ukraine began, the EU adopted the [REPowerEU](https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/policies/ending-russian-energy-imports/#0) plan, which contained several provisions, such as the shuttering of imports of Russian natural gas, pivoting towards renewable energy, and the authorization of gas imports from certain approved countries (this is a major issue plaguing boycotts of Russian goods; many are [sold through intermediary countries](https://eurasianet.org/new-report-documents-how-central-asian-states-abet-russian-sanctions-busting), particularly the Central Asian nations).  

The REPowerEU plan has proven to be effective in reducing Russian gas imports—while formerly Russia’s main receiver of natural gas, the EU receives only a [tiny sliver](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=66044) of Russian gas exports. However, some countries have [performed better](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/10/3/how-much-of-europes-oil-and-gas-still-comes-from-russia) in this metric than others. The countries of the [Netherlands](https://nltimes.nl/2026/05/13/netherlands-still-importing-liquefied-natural-gas-russia), [Belgium](https://en.highnorthnews.com/politics/eu-spent-72-billion-on-russian-lng-in-2025-maximizing-imports-before-2027-ban/1094229), and [France](https://www.kyivpost.com/post/68003) have all continued to export significant amounts of natural gas from Russia (although interestingly, they seem to have escaped international scrutiny, unlike [Germany](https://kjis.org/journal/view.html?uid=292&&vmd=Full)).   

However, these countries’ energy dependence pales in comparison to two others, which have completely [dropped the pretense](https://www.dw.com/en/russian-oil-flows-to-eu-through-ukraine-again/a-76894770) of an energy boycott (and are exempt from the Russian sanctions) and have firmly attached themselves to Russia’s oil and natural gas—[Hungary](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/ukrainealert/hungary-has-alternative-energy-options-but-chooses-to-rely-on-russia/) and [Slovakia](https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/slovakia-seeks-raise-russian-gas-imports-before-eu-ban-kicks-2026-03-06/).

##  **Slovakia: Populist Pandering**
Slovakia began as a strong supporter of Ukraine in the early days of the 2022 invasion and was [the first nation to send air-defense systems](https://apnews.com/article/slovakia-nato-ukraine-fighter-jets-weapons-7374f6219ed9edff190437621ec84bc1), but experienced a sharp turnaround as the country elected Prime Minister Robert Fico of the Direction – Social Democracy party. When he was elected he promised “[not to send a single cartridge](https://www.nytimes.com/2023/10/26/world/europe/slovakia-weapons-ukraine-war.html)” to Ukraine and has blocked EU funds to the nation. Fico has argued that such funding will lead to [further conflict and bloodshed within the conflict](https://english.nv.ua/russian-war/slovak-pm-fico-says-slovakia-could-monitor-ukraine-ceasefire-but-rejects-troop-deployment-50574118.html). He has also, unsurprisingly, furthered suppression of the media and targeted liberal institutions along with perceived political enemies who threaten his power.  

The EU, for obvious reasons, did not idly stand by as this all occurred, and in [May of 2026, issued a final warning to Fico](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/news/en/agenda/plenary-news/2026-05-18/8/slovakia-vote-on-the-rule-of-law-fundamental-rights-and-misuse-of-eu-funds) regarding the backsliding of democratic values and the rights of the citizens of Slovakia. Fico has dismissed the EU’s findings while attacking their leadership. Slovakia still has a long way to go, especially given its current government, but there is hope, especially considering what has happened in its neighbor, Hungary.

## **Hungary: A Cautious Hope**
With the current situation in Eastern Europe, democracy might seem hopeless, but Hungary proves that isn’t the case. Prime Minister Viktor Orbán ruled the nation for sixteen long years, turning the nation into an illiberal state by warping state media, manipulating the judiciary, eroding democratic safeguards, and cozying up with Russia, becoming Vladimir Putin’s best friend in the European Union.  He’s been frequently criticized in and outside of Europe. Additionally, the EU's requirement for unanimous voting meant that he was able to single-handedly veto many key policy decisions, which would have harmed Russia’s position, such as aid to Ukraine and EU enlargement.  

This would be changed on the 12th of April 2026, when Hungary had its parliamentary election, where record turnout occurred for the center-right Tisza Party led by Péter Magyar. Following a landslide victory with an outright 141-seat constitutional majority and his inauguration on May 9th, Magyar set out to undo some of the damage done by Orbán. His government has worked to undo the legal damage done and root out the oligarchic corruption and unrest that was caused by the previous administration. While not perfect, this victory shows that perhaps there is hope for better Eastern Europe.

## **Eastern Europe: At a Crossroads**
Eastern Europe remains on a tightrope, despite the major recent victory against Orbán. Russia still holds a pervasive influence over countless political parties, economic industries, and governments. Nations such as Bulgaria and [Slovenia](https://sloveniatimes.com/47267/new-speakers-nato-and-moscow-plans-raise-eyebrows) have formed pro-Russian-aligned governments, while [Romania](https://ecfr.eu/article/romanias-government-collapse-is-a-trap-with-no-easy-exit/) saw its pro-European government collapse recently, opening the doors for Russian exploitation.   

This is not to say that all is doom and gloom. Far from it, in fact. The power of the people is becoming evermore evident, and it is in their power to sway these Eastern European countries on the right course. Voters in Armenia recently [reelected](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cvgel990n51o) the incumbent prime minister, Nikol Pashinyan, despite severe pushback against his rapprochement with Azerbaijan and the West. This result demonstrates the will of the people, even in difficult circumstances (with Russia stripping Armenia of many of its trade privileges).  

The Russian protrusion into European politics *can* be stopped. But this requires a unified and firm response. Europe must stand against Russian violations of territorial sovereignty and its oligarchic and economic influence. The people of Europe can stand against Russia through the ballot, to ensure regimes like those in Slovakia are the next dominoes to fall.  

This also requires a response from people across the globe. We cannot remain silent while people in [Serbia](https://www.lemonde.fr/en/international/article/2026/05/24/new-wave-of-serbia-protests-draws-nearly-200-000-people_6753778_4.html), Bulgaria, and Georgia fight for their freedom against their regimes, while Ukraine stands defiant against the Russian onslaught.   

It is in the hands of the people, whether European or American or Asian or African, whether Eastern Europe will operate as a sovereign, free, democratic region, or if it will be swallowed by the Russian bear.
