Part of what most would consider the “American Dream” is [upwards social mobility](https://www.bushcenter.org/catalyst/state-of-the-american-dream/churchwell-history-of-the-american-dream) and education plays a large role in that. The traditionally presented narrative is that you go to college, get a worthwhile degree and find a stable job to support a family with a nice picket fence house. This is far from the case in modern American society with [2/3rds of Gen Z Americans](https://www.scotsmanguide.com/news/two-thirds-of-gen-z-think-they-will-never-own-a-home/) feeling that they will never be able to own a home despite many wanting to. Finding [work has become challenging](https://www.cnn.com/2026/06/01/economy/finding-new-job-challenges) despite increasing jobs for many and there is a public sentiment that [wages aren’t keeping up with our higher cost of living](https://www.shrm.org/topics-tools/news/benefits-compensation/workers-say-pay-isnt-keeping-up-with-cost-of-living), but at least we can take comfort in our valuable college education right?

Well for many Americans, higher education has become a significant financial burden. [Tuition costs have risen dramatically](https://www.npr.org/2025/11/20/nx-s1-5600854/college-costs-have-risen-dramatically-in-the-last-20-years-heres-why), especially for private institutions, over the past several decades, far outpacing inflation and wage growth. Students are often encouraged to pursue degrees with the [promise that their investment will pay off in the future](https://freopp.org/whitepapers/does-college-pay-off-a-comprehensive-return-on-investment-analysis/), yet millions graduate carrying significant debt while entering an increasingly competitive job market. There has also been the growing concern over academic freedoms and student rights with the current polarized political climate.

Throughout the last year, the White House has issued multiple overviews and statements on the Trump administration’s handling of education and initiatives to let education be a matter of the state and its people despite repeatedly attacking higher education institutions. During President Trump’s First [100 Days](https://www.ed.gov/about/initiatives/president-trumps-first-100-days-education-america) of his second term, multiple changes took action against the supposedly harmful “Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion” policies employed by colleges, initiating investigations into institutions such as Harvard and Columbia.

Stuyvesant students, like most American high schoolers, have an unfortunate tendency to correlate prestige with future academic or career merit which isn’t necessarily true. The pressure to attend highly ranked and often expensive universities can lead students to feel stressed and apply to schools which don’t necessarily align with their future goals.  

In fact, public universities and in-state institutions frequently offer strong academic programs at a fraction of the cost of elite private schools. Yet many students and families continue to view these alternatives as less desirable because of the cultural emphasis placed on prestige. The City College of New York was once described as the “Harvard of the Proletariat” and it’s clear why. The college boosts many well accomplished and successful individuals from diverse backgrounds and yet would be looked down upon by some.

The value of a college education should not be measured solely by a school’s ranking or brand name. What matters more is whether an institution provides students with the resources, opportunities, and support necessary to achieve their goals. For many of us, affordable public colleges, community colleges, and state universities can offer pathways to success that are just as effective as those provided by more prestigious institutions.

College should never be viewed as a luxury for the few who can afford it, and we should relearn the importance of uplifting every American to get the chance to pursue education if they so desire. The knowledge and skills gained from college education can and should be applied to a variety of jobs and it’s beneficial to the individual and whole society when we’re all more educated.
